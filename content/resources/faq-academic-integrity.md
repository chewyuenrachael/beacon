# FAQ — Academic Integrity and AI Assistants

## Does Cursor “do students’ homework”?

Cursor is an **editor with integrated assistance**. Like search, libraries, and office hours, it can be used well or poorly. Course policies—not the tool—define what is allowed on a given assignment.

## What should ambassadors say when faculty push back?

Acknowledge the concern. Offer **concrete syllabus language** patterns: require `git` history, written explanations of design tradeoffs, in-class checks, or restricted network modes for exams. Position Cursor as something to **disclose and document**, not hide.

## How do we avoid encouraging cheating at events?

- Demo on **public** or **synthetic** repos, never a live course solution set.
- Say explicitly: “If your syllabus says no AI on this project, that applies in Cursor too.”
- Highlight **reviewing diffs** and **running tests** as part of responsible use.

## What about group projects?

Encourage teams to agree on **tooling norms** up front: who runs Agent, how merges are reviewed, and how shared API keys are never pasted into chat.

## Where can students get clarity?

Direct them to their **instructor** and **TA** for the authoritative answer. Your role is to avoid contradicting institutional policy while still being honest that many students already use multiple assistants—norms help more than denial.
