# Professor Talk & Classroom Touchpoint Playbook

Faculty talks are **high leverage** when framed as pedagogy and tooling—not hype. Your north star: help them set norms that keep assignments fair and students productive.

## Positioning

- You are **not** pitching cheating—you are showing how IDEs already integrate assistance and how syllabi can **define allowed tools** clearly.
- Pair with **one concrete course artifact**: a homework autograder, a style guide, or a starter repo they already use.

## Slide outline (12–15 slides)

1. Title + who you are (campus ambassador / Cursor liaison).
2. What changed in CS pedagogy (short—1 slide).
3. Cursor in one sentence: editor + codebase-aware assistance.
4. Live demo: **read → edit → test** on their starter code (pre-negotiated file).
5. Academic integrity framing: compare to calculators, Stack Overflow, pair programming policies.
6. What instructors can require: “show `git` history,” “explain diff in prose,” “no cloud on this assignment.”
7. Student discount + support resources (FAQ links).
8. Offer: **office hours** for TAs to try the same workflow on a lab solution.
9. Q&A.

## Delivery tips

- **Rehearse** with their TA once; they will catch course-specific landmines.
- **Mute** notifications; use a **clean** Git branch named `demo/cursor-guest`.
- If challenged on fairness, pivot to **process**: drafts, tests, attribution—not banning tools that already exist on student laptops.

## Office hours follow-up (optional, 1 week later)

- 60 minutes, **TA-only** or **faculty + TAs**.
- Walk through grading a synthetic submission that used assistance badly vs well.
- Leave behind a **one-page policy template** they can paste into the syllabus appendix.

## Metrics that resonate

Share **anonymous** workshop stats if you have them (attendance, optional survey: “I understand when I may use AI on assignments” before/after). Avoid unverifiable adoption claims.
